week 2
prompt engineering curriculum:

### 1. Few-Shot Prompting

Providing the model with a few examples of input-output pairs to establish a pattern, tone, or format before asking for the final result.

### 2. Chain-of-Thought (CoT)

Asking the model to "think step-by-step." This is crucial for logic, math, or complex reasoning problems where the model needs to process intermediate steps to reach the correct conclusion.

### 3. Role Prompting (Persona Adoption)

Assigning the LLM a specific identity—like "You are a Senior DevOps Engineer" or "You are a world-class legal researcher"—to narrow the domain of its response and improve accuracy.

### 4. Delimiters and Structural Formatting

Using clear markers like triple quotes (`"""`), XML tags (`<context></context>`), or Markdown headers to separate instructions from the data the model needs to process.

### 5. Constraint and Negative Prompting

Explicitly defining what the model **must** include (e.g., "Response must be under 50 words") and what it **must not** do (e.g., "Do not use technical jargon").

### 6. Zero-Shot Chain-of-Thought

Simply adding the phrase "Let's think step by step" to a prompt to trigger better reasoning without providing manual examples.

Problem #2 - Sentiment Analysis

Type: sentiment analysis
Input: That was FINE example of teaching an intelligent automation class. :-/
Output: the emotions the text conveys


Note- upon google searching for this  :-/  it stated that it was an emoticon representing a skeptical, uncertain, or hesitant facial expression, often conveying mixed emotions, mild displeasure, disappointment, or awkwardness.

Analysis:

As this problem deals with Sentiment Analysis and this particular statement deals with a sentence and an emoticon.

For the below sentence
That was FINE example... :-/

It can be split into 2 parts - Primary and Secondary

Primary Emotions

These are the first, automatic reactions to a situation. They are often instinctive, universal, and brief. In the context of the prompt engineering problem:

    The Stimulus: A class that was perhaps poorly taught or frustrating.
    The Primary Emotion: Likely disappointment or frustration. This is the core feeling that arose immediately after the experience.
    Common Examples: Joy, sadness, fear, anger, surprise, and disgust.

Secondary Emotions

These are the emotional reactions to the primary emotion. They are often more complex, learned, and can act as a "mask" for the original feeling. They are shaped by our thoughts, social expectations, or defense mechanisms.

    The Expression: "That was FINE example... :-/"
    The Secondary Emotion: Sarcasm or Cynicism. Instead of stating "I am disappointed," the user uses irony to distance themselves from the vulnerability of being upset.
    Why they matter in AI: An LLM that only identifies "Positive" (because of the word "fine") or "Negative" misses the secondary layer of sarcasm, which is the most important part of the communication.



Prompt Technique:
To address this, I propose to ensure the LLM captures the underlying subtext rather than just the literal definitions.
Strategy: Few-Shot Prompting with Emotional Mapping


Prompt: You are a linguistics expert specializing in digital communication. Analyze the following inputs and identify the primary and secondary emotions conveyed, paying close attention to capitalization and emoticons.

Input: "I am so much relieved that we could finally make it :)"
Output: Primary: Kindness, Secondary: Relief

Input:"Did I have a good time? Definitely :/
Output:Primary: Thoughtful, Secondary: Sarcasm

Input:"That was FINE example of teaching an intelligent automation class. :-/"
Output:

The output llm gave was 
Primary: Sarcasm, Secondary: Disapproval




Problem #3 - Simple Math

Type: text generation and reasoning
Output: the sum of 2,292.32 and 4494 1/3
Rules: return only the result and make the result a fraction

Prompt Technique:

The objective is to ensure that a Large Language Model correctly processes different numerical types—a decimal and a mixed number and performs addition without losing precision through floating-point approximations. The following prompts utilize specialized techniques to achieve the required fractional output.

Prompt 1: Chain of Thought Reasoning

Perform the following calculation by following each step exactly.

    Convert the decimal 2,292.32 into its simplest fractional form.
    Convert the mixed number  4494 1/3 into an improper fraction.
    Add the fractions together to find the final sum.
    Provide only the final result as a single improper fraction.


Inference:
In this strategy, CoT serves as a logic gate. By mandating the conversion of 2,292.32 to 57308/25 and 4,49431​ to 13483/3, the model establishes a precise symbolic baseline. The subsequent steps of adding and providing the final result ensures the desired outcome is met.
